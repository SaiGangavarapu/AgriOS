# Farming Systems Comparison

## 1. Principle

AgriOS must not frame farming systems as a simplistic conflict between “chemical” and “chemical-free.” Each system has production, labour, nutrient, certification, transition, and risk implications.

## 2. Conventional input-based farming

Characteristics:

- mineral fertilizer
- chemical crop-protection inputs
- improved or hybrid varieties
- mechanization
- irrigation where available

Strengths:

- predictable nutrient concentration
- rapid correction of major deficiencies
- compatibility with high-yield systems
- established supply chains

Risks:

- blanket application
- nutrient imbalance
- input dependency
- environmental loss
- residue or safety violations
- weak soil-organic-matter management

AgriOS requirement: enforce soil-test-based, crop-stage-based, weather-aware, and legally compliant use.

## 3. Integrated farming

Combines:

- soil-test-based fertilizer
- manure and compost
- crop residues
- legumes
- biofertilizers
- rotation
- integrated pest management
- water conservation

This is frequently the most practical transition path because it aims to maintain production while improving input efficiency and soil function.

## 4. Organic farming

Characteristics:

- standards-defined permitted inputs
- certification and conversion period
- strong traceability
- contamination controls
- restricted synthetic inputs

Operational requirements:

- approved input register
- field history
- buffer zones
- audit records
- segregated harvest and storage
- certification renewal

## 5. Natural and low-external-input farming

Potential elements:

- on-farm biomass recycling
- mulching
- farmyard manure
- compost
- green manure
- biological preparations
- mixed cropping
- cover crops
- reduced purchased inputs

Critical constraint: nutrients removed in harvested produce must ultimately be replenished. Plans must include nutrient-budget monitoring rather than assume that avoiding purchased fertilizer automatically maintains fertility.

## 6. Conservation agriculture

Core themes:

- minimum soil disturbance
- permanent or substantial soil cover
- crop diversification

Suitability depends on:

- crop system
- machinery
- residue availability
- weed management
- soil and rainfall conditions

## 7. Precision agriculture

Uses:

- field zoning
- sensors
- satellite or drone observations
- variable recommendations
- automated or assisted control

Precision agriculture is a management method, not a farming ideology. It can support conventional, integrated, organic, and natural systems.

## 8. Comparison dimensions

Every plan should compare:

- input cost
- labour
- machinery
- yield range
- transition risk
- nutrient sufficiency
- water demand
- pest-management options
- certification needs
- market premium
- knowledge requirement
- evidence confidence

## 9. Platform position

AgriOS should let farmers select a target pathway and produce a safe, field-specific plan. The system must disclose uncertainty and must not promise guaranteed yield or premium pricing.
